Front-End brown bag - Flexbox basics

Updated March 2017

Intro

Before Flexbox, we had HTML Flow. Actually, we still have HTML Flow, so now we have both. In order to understand the ways that Flexbox is different from HTML Flow, it helps to understand HTML Flow.

What's HTML Flow?

To understand HTML Flow, first picture a big blank page on a screen. Then imagine that there's a giant invisible magnet in the upper left corner. Everything you place on this big empty page will be sucked as closely as it can get to the upper left corner, unless there's already something else in the way (you can change this behavior, but that's the default).

Next you have to understand inline and block behavior and the differences between them. HTML has a number of "tags" to markup content, and they can all basically be divided into either inline tags, like <span> or <b> or <em>, or block tags, like <div> or <p> or <h1> (the <table> tag is its own thing).

Inline-level behavior

An inline object, as the name sort of implies, basically behaves like a line of text. For example, if you take a simple text file, stick a .html extension on it, and type a sentence in it: 


No matter where you go, there you are.

it'll look like this in the browser:

No matter where you go, there you are.

If you type a really really long sentence in the file, so long that it's longer than the width of your browser and runs out of room, it'll look like this (depending on the width of your browser window):

Hearken if you will to the tale of the fall of a lord of darkness, and the return of a king of light. Concern yourselves with armies and wizards, phantoms and emperors, cloud-capp'd towers, and bloodied fields of horrendous carnage.

That's inline behavior - if your page contains nothing but a bunch of inline objects, they'll line up with the first object as far up and to the left as possible on the page, the next one will follow immediately after it on the same line, and so on. If the line of objects runs out of space, it will wrap to the next line, with the next object again aligning itself as far left and up as possible, and the rest arraying themselves after it along the same line.

To prove this, let's put the content inside a bunch of inline tags: 


<span>All that is gold does not glitter,</span>
<a href="#mynameisurl">not all those who wander are lost,</a>
<em>the old that is strong does not wither,</em>
<code>deep roots are not reached by the frost.</code>

All that is gold does not glitter, not all those who wander are lost, the old that is strong does not wither, deep roots are not reached by the frost.

Even though each line of this stanza is wrapped in a different tag, and they are each arranged on their own line in the markup, the entire verse is rendered as a single string of text by the browser (along with the default browser styling for that tag) that wraps to the next line when it runs out of space.

Block-level behavior

A block object, as the name sort of implies, basically behaves like a box. A crazy, magical box.

Imagine a box that is automatically as wide as its container and yet has no height. Such a box would occupy its own line in the flow, pushing everything after it to the next line. Take a look at this markup: 


<span>This sentence is inside an inline-level tag.</span>
<div>This sentence is inside a block-level tag.</div>
<div class="empty-box"></div>
<div>This sentence is inside a block-level tag.</div>
<span>This sentence is inside an inline-level tag.</span>

There's an empty div in between the two other divs. See in the example below how it has no height and is therefore invisible. See also how the two inline-level spans each occupy their own lines because each block-level div is selfishly occupying the line it's sitting on.

Messing with the behavior

You can change all this behavior, thanks to CSS. For example, you can change the location of the giant invisible magnet to the upper right corner: 


body {
  direction: rtl;
}

You can make something "float" to one side of its container so the rest of the content flows around it like a river: 


<img class="matt-foley" src="matt-foley.jpg">
<p>"I am 35 years old, eating a steady diet of government cheese, thrice divorced, and living in a van DOWN BY THE RIVER!" &mdash;Matt Foley</p>

"I am 35 years old, eating a steady diet of government cheese, thrice divorced, and living in a van DOWN BY THE RIVER!" —Matt Foley

If we float the image left: 


.matt-foley {
  float: left;
}

we get this:

"I am 35 years old, eating a steady diet of government cheese, thrice divorced, and living in a van DOWN BY THE RIVER!" —Matt Foley

You can make things appear visually offset from where they are actually located by altering their position relative to their natural location in the HTML flow: 


.matt-foley {
  position: relative;
  top: -20px;
  left: -20px;
}

"I am 35 years old, eating a steady diet of government cheese, thrice divorced, and living in a van DOWN BY THE RIVER!" —Matt Foley

You can even take things completely out of the flow, so that everything else on the page acts like it's not even there: 


.matt-foley {
  position: absolute;
}

So that's HTML Flow - basically, when you set something to block level, you're telling it to behave like a box, and when you set it to inline level, you're telling it to behave like text.

Awesome! Then why do we even need Flexbox?

While HTML has come a long way and can be used to create amazing layouts and designs, that's not what it was originally for. HTML was intended to easily create scientific and engineering documents, and we've learned to trick it into making, well, pretty pictures - and now, user interfaces for actual applications.

Flexbox was developed to overcome some of the shortcomings of HTML Flow, mainly vertical and horizontal alignment, and overflow of content within containers that can shrink or grow as needed. This is a more conducive box model for the development of user interfaces than regular ol' HTML Flow.

How does Flexbox work?

Let's find out how Flexbox works by starting simple and working our way up to the complicated stuff. Checkout this simple nested divs object: 


<div class="container">
  <div class="child one"></div>
  <div class="child two"></div>
  <div class="child three"></div>
</div>

No need to show what this looks like because, as we learned earlier, since these divs are empty they have no height and are therefore invisible. So let's sprinkle some CSS on them: 


.container {
  border: 10px solid #999;
}

  .container .child {
    width: 100px;
    height: 100px;
  }

  .container .one   { background-color: blue; }
  .container .two   { background-color: yellow; }
  .container .three { background-color: red; }

Since each div is block-level they each occupy their own line, per HTML Flow. Now let's make the container a "flexbox" by changing its display property from block (the default) to flex: 


.container {
  display: flex;
  ...

What the hell just happened? The child divs are no longer acting like block-level objects. Did their display properties get changed? Let's do some experimenting to check. We'll remove the display style from .container (setting it back to the default block), and set the child divs to display: inline;:

Ugh, no - even though the child divs have a height set, it doesn't do anything when they're set to display: inline because you can't change the "height" of text (not directly). So they're invisible. Let's try display: inline-block, which causes the element to behave like text, but lets you apply width, height, margin and padding:

This sort of looks like the flexbox example, except we've got this weird whitespace along the right and bottom of each div. This is happening because the child divs are inline and are therefore being treated as text by the browser. The space to the right of each child div is the natural spacing between letters. As for the spacing under each child div: text characters can include "descenders" (like the letters p and q and g) and so the browser automatically leaves room for those descenders so they don't run over any "ascenders" (like the letters h and l and k) that might be on the next line below.

So it's not either of those display values. We can of course determine definitively by checking our browser's inspector, which will reveal that those child divs still have a display value of block. So why aren't they acting like block-level objects by occupying their own lines per HTML Flow? The answer is that they are the immediate children of a "flexbox" (a container whose display property is set to flex), which makes them, in flexbox parlance, "flex items" and therefore they are no longer handling their own arrangement and positioning - the flexbox container is.

It's important to note here that, although the child divs are flex items located inside a flexbox, the flex behavior doesn't cascade - the child divs are still block-level, so any children they might have will still follow normal HTML Flow. If you want the children inside the child divs to behave like flex items, you would need to set the child divs to display: flex; as well.

So let's look at our flexbox again:

We can see that, by setting only one declaration (display: flex; on .container), the container arranges the child divs neatly in a row. The container still behaves like a normal block-level element - it is occupying its entire line, and its height is being determined by its content (the three child divs), otherwise, it would be invisible (except for the border we applied). So it's really only about managing the arrangement of its immediate children.

Let's experiment by setting the width of the container to a value that's less than the combined width of the children (100px * 3 = 300px), so like 220px. In normal HTML Flow, depending on the styles being applied, we'd expect the last child div to either wrap to the next line, or perhaps "overflow" past the right edge of the container.
